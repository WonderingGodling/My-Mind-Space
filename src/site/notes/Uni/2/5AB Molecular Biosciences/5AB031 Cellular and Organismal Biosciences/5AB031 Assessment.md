##### halophytes (Mangrove)
- Mainly in saltwater
	- like coastlines and tidal rivers
- Red mangroves exclude salt by having significantly impermeable roots that are highly suberised (impregnated with [suberin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suberin "Suberin")), acting as an ultrafiltration mechanism to exclude sodium salts from the rest of the plant.
	- One study found that roots of the Indian mangrove Avicennia officinalis exclude 90% to 95% of the salt in water taken up by the plant (Krishnamurthy et al., 2014)
- restrict the opening of their [stomata](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stomata)
- vary the orientation of their leaves to avoid the harsh midday sun and so reduce evaporation from the leaves
- A captive red mangrove grows only if its leaves are misted with fresh water several times a week, simulating frequent tropical rainstorms
- The root possesses a hierarchical, triple layered pore structure in the [epidermis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epidermis_\(botany\) "Epidermis (botany)") and most Na+ ions are filtered at each layer 
	- mostly the first
- Uptake of Na+ ions is desirable for halophytes to build up osmotic potential, absorb water and sustain turgor pressure.
- Halophytes exclude salt through their roots, secrete the accumulated salt through their aerial parts and sequester salt in senescent leaves and/or the bark.
