# <center><span style="color:#5E2C2A">Functional Significance Of Sexual Coercion</span></center>

## Introduction
Across sexually reproducing taxa there are various methods that are implemented to attract the opposite sex such as courtship displays (Mitoyen, Quigley and Fusani, 2019). The techniques used to secure a mating opportunity are ultimately to manipulate or make use of the individuals reproductive success and fitness. One of the factors that goes into reproductive success is female choice, the idea that the females in a population are the deciders of which males pass on their genes to their progeny (Mitoyen, Quigley and Fusani, 2019). In some animals individuals (typically the males of the species) make use of physical, psychological/social or behavioural methods of manipulating otherwise reluctant females into choosing them (Sentenská, Šedo and Pekár, 2020). The exchange of services (McFarland and Majolo, 2011), defence (Pizzari, 2001), and agression (Burke and Holwell, 2021) are some examples. For the coercive participant this often provides social benefits, limits Intrasexual competition, bypasses the luring and persuading parts of courtship displays, and reduces risk of harm—making frequent mating attempts easier as well as less costly to the male often eat the expense of the female (Sentenská, Šedo and Pekár, 2020;Grayson et al., 2012).

There are more direct advantages for the reproductive fitness of the performer of these tactics such as being able to mate for longer in the moment which increases the chance of successful fertilisation (Sentenská, Šedo and Pekár, 2020). Though the recipient of behaviour that prolongs copulation can be affected by limb damage  as an example of how coercive behaviour is harmful to to the individual on the other side of it(Sentenská, Šedo and Pekár, 2020). 

## Main Body
### Coercion
The main classifications of coercive behaviour are forced copulation, harassment, and intimidation (Sentenská, Šedo and Pekár, 2020). Animals in a population will exchange goods and services between each other to increase individual and inclusive fitness. How an individual acquires a service can be reliant on its value to the individual and how likely it is for them to be able to take it buy force (McFarland and Majolo, 2011). Coercion tactics, such as aggression, allow for the more dominant members of a population to get a service or to stop others' behaviours to the individuals own benefit. If a conflict begins the individuals involved are more likely to be on the receiving end of further aggressive behaviour which shows that coercion can also be a continuous event (McFarland and Majolo, 2011) as hostility caused by a refusal to mate could lead to fewer future rejections (Clutton-Brock and Parker, 1995). ### Its Evolution
Sexual traits (physical and behavioural) co-evolve with each other and are often at odds (Mazzi et al., 2009). Due to gestation periods, and other costs associated with carrying and baring offspring, in most species, males are able to reproduce more than females (Gage et al., 2002). For this reason female evolutionary development tends to optimise mate quality while males tend to optimise for mate quantity creating sexual conflict. 
When there is a higher proportion of either sexually competing males or females (oporational sex ratio), the smaller collective experiences drawbacks (Grayson et al., 2012). Sexual conflict allows the introduction of sex biased traits and the introduction of cost-controlling traits as a means of defence (Mazzi et al., 2009), with males usually evolving aggressive mating behaviours to compete for the limited availability of fertile females, and females evolving behaviours to help avoid exposure to male aggression. Figure 2 shows some examples of behaviour demonstrated (Grayson et al., 2012).

Since reproductive success increases when a male can copulate with more females without those females mating again with another male, the lower the males mating costs the more females it will mate with, and if males can mate with a high enough quantity of females then it becomes benefitial to make rejection more costly for them (Clutton-Brock and Parker, 1995).

When the operational sex ratio is skewed, traits that increase an individual's (or its progeny's) mating success are primarily favoured (Grayson et al., 2012). For example male heavy ratios tend to cause more competition and fighting as well as a more costly mating process. If, however it is balanced cryptic female choice acts as a driving force for reproductive traits and is a controlling factor in which genes persist (Gage et al., 2002). This fuels intrasexual conflict and results in a more diverse set of reproductive traits/strategies between males of a species as a way to override female preference in place of operational sex ratio and creates different niches in the gene pool. This balance also means that operational sex ratio reflects sperm competition (Mazzi et al., 2009).

### Cost-Benefit Balance
In red-spotted newts, *Notophthalmus viridescens*, males (sometimes multiple as females can accept multiple spermatophores) grasp uninterested females prior to copulation then secret hormones that make them more receptive to mating (Grayson et al., 2012).  This process can act as female defence or mate guarding  (protecting the female from predators and competing males) but can also cause a stress response that supresses the females immune system and results in weight loss.

### In Action
In "Biting and binding: an exclusive coercive mating strategy of males in a philodromid spider" Sentenská and pekar found that _Thanatus fabricii_, also known as Fabrici's ground running spider, has been known to bite females and bind their legs using silk as a precursor to their mating process. Starting with this results in the female being paralysed during mating which increases the length of time the male can attempt reproduction (Figure 3) consequently increasing the chance of reproductive success.

This method of coercion is very beneficial to the male running spider as it is less energetically demanding and less vulnerable to overpower the female instead of attempting to court her—most spider sexual interactions are dominated by the female and very often end (or start) with the male being injured or cannibalised. The male, being safer during copulation, has evolved to have larger venom glands than female (or than closely related males) and will bite females for longer if they are perceived to be stronger of more agile.

Conversely, after this the receiving female is still rendered immobile for a while. Leaving her vulnerable to predators and unable to find food (which under normal circumstances would be the male spider) which means that the female receives less nutrition. This nutritional deficit becomes an ongoing issue as they found that after the male successfully mates with the female they struggle to catch prey as easily as before (Figure 4).

Though very few insects exhibit sexually coercive behaviour, females being physically impaired as a result is common among many animals. Male sea otters grasp their partners with their claws or teeth often resulting in drowning or permanent injury(Clutton-Brock and Parker, 1995). The male *Schizocosa ocreat*, brush-legged wolf spider, uses its fangs to grasps and mount females and _Miomantis caffra_ will fight with each other before copulating. Both of these lead to blood loss and scarring  for the females and cannibalisation for losing males (Sentenská, Šedo and Pekár, 2020; Burke and Holwell, 2021). Female mantises of that species can reproduce asexually to avoid the damage that causes.

### Counter Measures
To avoid the costly nature of coercion females develop mitigative behaviour (Smit, 2025). For example *Drosophila montana*, fruit fly, mating involves females attempting to 'buck off' males and males attempting to prolong copulation for as long as they can (Mazzi et al., 2009). *Gallus gallus*, Red junglefowl,  let out a sound of distress when they are forcefully mated with to direct competitive male attention (Pizzari, 2001). The similarity in these cases is the inevitability of copulation. Where copulation and its costs are unavoidable the recipient tends to aim for a shortening in the length of copulation. This decreases the success rate. Since female optima is often towards quality over quantity (Gage et al., 2002) the male's status, and the female's resistance are put at odds making it unlikely that a low ranking or unfit male will be able to successfully reproduce. (Pizzari, 2001)

### Generally
Males that have less time (imminently or existentially) will favour speed and/or quantity of mating meaning that forced copulation is more common when there is high intrasexual competition or predation (Clutton-Brock and Parker, 1995; Sentenská, Šedo and Pekár, 2020). It is rare in polygyny because it is more energetically costly than it is beneficial whereas polyandry mating costs are usually higher for females. It can benefit either sex but the cost trend makes the technique harmful (to the female) in some species but optimal in others (Gage et al., 2002). similarly, the costs of repeated courtship can apply to both sexes but affects females more the larger the males are (by comparison) and so is seen more in polygyny than polyandry (Clutton-Brock and Parker, 1995).

Wild barbary macaques show a post-copulatory behaviour, reconciliation. After conflict the victim will show attempts at reconciliation by offering the aggressor services (McFarland and Majolo, 2011). This often reduces the risk of being the target of aggression again, and rejecting the aggressor again can often lead to a repeat of their aggression. The value of relationship repair is dependant on how beneficial the individual perceives the relationship as being. 

## Conclusion

Sexual coercion is a variety of male behaviour that many taxa display, including intimidation, harassment, sequestering and infanticide. Coercion developed as a way of overcoming cryptic female choice to increase sexual fitness at the cost of the recipients instead of the contributors. The existence of coercion allows for variation despite female preference and improves the reproductive fitness of the coercer and increases its genes prevalence.
